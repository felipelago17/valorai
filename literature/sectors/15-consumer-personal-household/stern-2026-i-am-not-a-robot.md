## Summary

Journalist Joanna Stern spent a year delegating as much of her daily life as possible to AI tools — health monitoring, financial decisions, food sourcing, driving, childcare research, and personal relationships. A key organising principle (framed in the book as a rule: track costs) is that the costs of AI extend well beyond subscription fees: time spent correcting errors, privacy exposure from data shared with multiple providers, environmental load from the underlying infrastructure, employment effects on workers displaced by AI-enabled services, cognitive effects of delegating judgement, and ambiguous wellbeing effects of AI companionship. Subscription creep across a full personal AI stack is documented as a financial-TCO issue in its own right.

## Caveats / limits

Single-author, first-person account; findings are illustrative rather than statistically generalisable. Primarily an experiential rather than empirical source. No long passages should be quoted directly.
