# Labour & Supply Chain

## Definition

The performance of AI systems depends on human labour that is largely invisible to the organisations that procure and deploy them. This labour — data annotation, content moderation, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), quality assurance — is typically performed by workers in distributed, low-wage, often precarious employment arrangements. It is the hidden substrate of AI capability.

The indirect cost for organisations is not primarily a matter of paying these workers directly — most organisations do not. It is a matter of the ESG, reputational, legal, and regulatory exposure that accrues when the labour conditions of their AI supply chain become visible.

## The Evidence

Gray and Suri (2019) coined the term **ghost work** for the distributed human labour that powers AI and digital platforms. Their research documented the scale, conditions, and economic precariousness of this workforce: workers performing tasks through platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk, Scale AI, and numerous national equivalents, earning piece-rate wages that typically fall well below living standards in their locations, with no employment protections, benefits, or recourse.

The relevance to AI valuation is that this labour is not a historical artefact of early AI development. It is a structural feature of current AI systems. Large language models are trained using RLHF — a process in which human raters evaluate model outputs, providing the feedback signal that shapes the model's behaviour. Content moderation, required to prevent AI systems from producing harmful outputs, is performed by workers exposed to large volumes of graphic and disturbing content under time pressure and without adequate psychological support. These conditions have been the subject of regulatory investigations, litigation, and investigative journalism.

The categories of exposure that result include:

**ESG and investor scrutiny.** Institutional investors with ESG mandates increasingly require disclosure of supply-chain labour practices. AI supply chains that include ghost-work contractors operating under substandard conditions may fail these disclosures.

**Reputational risk.** Investigative reporting on AI labour practices reaches consumers and business partners. Organisations whose AI tools are visibly implicated in poor labour conditions face reputational consequences that can affect talent acquisition, customer relationships, and regulatory treatment.

**Legal and regulatory exposure.** Supply-chain due diligence legislation — including the German Supply Chain Due Diligence Act, the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, and analogous frameworks being developed in other jurisdictions — is expanding the scope of corporate liability for the conditions of workers in supply chains. AI supply chains will fall within these frameworks as regulatory coverage extends.

**Moderation harm and liability.** Content moderators who suffer psychological harm as a result of their working conditions have brought litigation against AI companies and platforms. As awareness of these conditions grows and legal frameworks develop, liability exposure for organisations that procure AI services built on inadequately supported moderation workforces may increase.

## How This Enters the Valuation

Labour and supply-chain costs should appear in the TCO model as:

- **Supply-chain audit and due-diligence cost** — the cost of understanding, documenting, and verifying the labour practices in the AI supply chain, required for ESG disclosure and regulatory compliance.
- **Remediation provision** — budget for improving conditions, switching suppliers, or managing the regulatory and reputational consequences of adverse findings.
- **ESG compliance overhead** — legal, reporting, and third-party assurance costs associated with supply-chain disclosure obligations.
- **Reputational risk premium** — this is harder to quantify but can be approached using structured expert judgement or scenario analysis: what is the probability of material reputational damage from supply-chain exposure, and what would that damage cost to remediate?

The financial materiality of this cost category varies significantly by organisation size, industry, and jurisdictional exposure. For large enterprises in regulated sectors, it is not trivial.
