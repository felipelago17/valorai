# Cognitive & Human Capital

## Definition

AI valuation models project productivity gains from AI-augmented staff. Implicit in those projections is an assumption that the staff who use AI tools retain the skills, judgement, and tacit knowledge that made them valuable before AI augmentation. A growing body of research challenges this assumption.

The mechanisms by which sustained AI use erodes cognitive and organisational capital include: **deskilling** (the atrophy of skills that are no longer exercised), **automation bias** (the tendency to accept AI outputs without adequate critical scrutiny), **cognitive offloading** (the delegation of cognitive tasks to AI in ways that reduce the operator's own mental engagement), and **over-reliance** (systematic deference to AI recommendations even when they are demonstrably incorrect).

These are not primarily technical problems. They are human and organisational ones — which is precisely why they tend not to appear in AI business cases authored by technical teams.

## The Evidence

**Critical thinking.** Gerlich (2025) surveyed 666 participants across a range of AI usage levels and found a statistically significant negative correlation between frequency of AI tool use and performance on critical-thinking assessments. Participants who used AI tools most intensively showed the most pronounced decline in independent reasoning capacity. The causal mechanism proposed is cognitive offloading: when AI handles the cognitive work, the operator's own capacity for that work degrades.

**Deskilling in professional contexts.** Natali and colleagues (2025) conducted a mixed-method review of AI-induced deskilling in medicine, finding that clinicians who relied on AI diagnostic tools showed measurable declines in their independent diagnostic accuracy and expressed reduced confidence in their clinical judgement over time. Automation bias — the tendency to accept AI-generated diagnoses without appropriate scrutiny — was identified as a compounding mechanism. While medicine is not representative of all AI deployment contexts, the pattern is likely to generalise to any domain in which AI tools are used for tasks that previously required expert judgement.

**Automation bias.** Automation bias is well-documented across aviation, healthcare, and industrial-control settings: operators exposed to AI or automated decision-support systems over-trust those systems' recommendations, particularly under time pressure or cognitive load. The consequence is that errors introduced by the AI system are less likely to be caught than equivalent errors made by a human, because the human reviewer's critical faculty is partly disengaged.

**Tacit knowledge erosion.** Much of the valuable judgement in organisations is tacit — it cannot be fully articulated or documented, and it is acquired through practice. When AI tools take over the activities through which tacit knowledge is built and maintained, the organisational stock of that knowledge declines. This is not immediately visible in productivity metrics, but it becomes visible when novel situations arise for which the AI has no training signal and human judgement must substitute.

## Why This Matters for Valuation

AI valuations that project sustained productivity gains implicitly assume that human capabilities are held constant. This assumption fails in predictable ways:

1. As AI use intensifies, the skills AI was augmenting begin to atrophy.
2. As skills atrophy, the organisation becomes more dependent on AI, reducing its ability to function when AI is unavailable or incorrect.
3. As organisational knowledge erodes, the quality of oversight degrades — the verification tax discussed in [Verification & Oversight](verification-oversight.md) increases because reviewers are less capable of detecting errors.

This creates a compounding dynamic that is not captured in linear productivity models.

## How This Enters the Valuation

Cognitive and human-capital costs should appear in the TCO model as:

- **Knowledge management overhead** — investment in retaining organisational knowledge that is at risk of being offloaded to AI: documentation, mentoring structures, practice communities, rotation programmes.
- **Deskilling provision** — budget for retraining staff whose skills have atrophied and who need to recover capability, particularly in preparation for periods of AI unavailability.
- **Reduced benefit realisation over time** — productivity projections should not be assumed to remain constant; a declining-benefit trajectory may more accurately reflect the human-capital dynamics at play.
- **Automation-bias incident costs** — provision for errors that result from over-reliance on AI outputs in consequential decisions.
