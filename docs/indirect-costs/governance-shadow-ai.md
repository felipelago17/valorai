# Governance & Shadow AI

## Definition

Shadow AI refers to the use of AI tools by employees outside the sanctioned technology stack — personal accounts on consumer AI services, browser extensions, third-party automation tools — that process organisational data and incorporate AI outputs into work products without any governance oversight. It is the AI equivalent of shadow IT, and it operates at a scale that dwarfs its historical predecessor.

MIT NANDA's *The GenAI Divide* (2025) quantifies the shadow AI economy: approximately **90% of workers** in surveyed organisations use personal AI tools at work, while only approximately **40%** use officially provisioned tools. The gap — roughly 50 percentage points — represents a governance blind spot of unusual magnitude.

## The Evidence

The NANDA findings establish three overlapping risks:

**Data leakage.** When employees use personal AI tool accounts to process organisational data, that data leaves the organisation's control. Consumer AI services may use data submitted in free-tier interactions to improve their models; terms of service often permit this. Sensitive commercial information, personal data subject to data protection law, client confidential information, and material non-public information may all be transmitted to and processed by systems with no data processing agreements, no access controls, and no deletion obligations.

**Intellectual property uncertainty.** AI-generated outputs produced using organisational data on personal accounts may not be owned by the organisation. The intellectual property status of AI-generated work is already legally unsettled; the shadow AI context adds jurisdictional complexity and raises questions about whether organisational outputs can be asserted as proprietary.

**Compliance and audit gaps.** Regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, legal, pharmaceuticals) operate under obligations to document the basis for consequential decisions. When AI tools are used outside governance frameworks, no audit trail exists. If a regulator requires the organisation to demonstrate how a decision was made, the answer cannot include undocumented AI tool use. The cost of reconstructing decision rationales — or of regulatory action where reconstruction is impossible — can be significant.

The NANDA framework also identifies a **learning gap** that compounds the governance risk: organisations that have not formalised AI governance have not invested in helping employees use AI tools effectively, calibrate their trust in AI outputs, or apply appropriate verification. Shadow AI users are therefore more likely to over-rely on AI outputs, less likely to detect errors, and less well-positioned to maintain the critical-thinking capacity discussed in [Cognitive & Human Capital](cognitive-human-capital.md).

## Why the Gap Is Larger Than It Appears

The 90% vs. 40% figures from NANDA are self-reported. Actual shadow AI usage is likely higher, for two reasons. First, social desirability bias: employees may under-report personal tool use if they believe it is prohibited. Second, definitional scope: the figures capture active tool use but not passive exposure to AI-generated content produced by colleagues and incorporated into shared work products without disclosure.

The governance gap is also dynamic — it widens as consumer AI capabilities improve and personal tool adoption increases faster than organisational provisioning. Organisations that have not addressed shadow AI governance are running behind a moving target.

## How This Enters the Valuation

Governance and shadow AI costs should appear in the TCO model as:

- **Shadow AI discovery and inventory** — the cost of understanding the AI tools currently in use across the organisation, typically requiring tooling (DLP solutions, network monitoring) and staff time.
- **Policy development and enforcement** — legal and HR cost of developing an AI acceptable-use policy, communicating it, and establishing enforcement mechanisms.
- **Data-breach and incident provision** — actuarial estimate of the probability and cost of a data-leakage incident attributable to shadow AI use, based on the organisation's data sensitivity, employee count, and existing DLP posture.
- **Compliance remediation** — in regulated sectors, provision for regulatory action arising from undocumented AI use in consequential decisions.
- **Governance tooling** — AI governance platforms, access controls, and audit infrastructure required to bring AI use within the organisational compliance perimeter.

The business case for governance investment is not merely defensive. Organisations that understand their AI use landscape are better positioned to direct investment toward sanctioned tools that deliver value, and to identify the shadow use that reflects unmet needs addressable through official provision.
